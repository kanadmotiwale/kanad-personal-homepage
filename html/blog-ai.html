AI Tools That Are Genuinely Changing How I Work

March 2026 · 7 min read

Everyone has an opinion on AI right now. Mine is pretty simple: the tools are genuinely useful, the hype around them is not. I have spent the last year integrating different AI tools into my workflow as a software engineer and grad student, and I want to share what has made a real difference versus what felt impressive for five minutes and then got abandoned.

Coding Assistants: The Real Story

GitHub Copilot was the first tool that actually changed my daily workflow. The value is not in generating complete features. It is in the small stuff: filling in boilerplate, suggesting the next line of a loop, autocompleting repetitive test cases. It saves maybe 20 to 30 minutes on a typical coding day, and that adds up fast.

Cursor takes this further by treating the whole codebase as context. When I am refactoring a service with multiple files, Cursor understands how they connect and can make changes that stay consistent across all of them. It is not magic but it reduces the cognitive overhead of holding the whole system in your head at once.

The AI Tools Worth Your Time

GitHub Copilot

In-editor autocomplete. Integrates directly into VS Code and JetBrains.

Cursor

AI-first code editor. Excellent for multi-file refactoring and codebase-aware suggestions.

Claude

Best for longer reasoning, explaining codebases, and drafting technical documentation.

ChatGPT

Great for quick lookups, syntax questions, and rubber duck debugging.

Perplexity

AI-powered search with citations. Useful for researching and comparing libraries.

Notion AI

Useful for design documents, meeting notes, and summarizing long technical specs.

Midjourney

For generating UI mockup inspiration and presentation visuals without a designer.

Whisper / Otter.ai

Accurate transcription for turning recorded meetings into searchable notes.

How AI Has Changed My Study Habits

As a grad student, the biggest shift has been in how I engage with dense academic material. When I am reading a distributed systems paper and hit a concept I do not fully understand, I no longer spend 30 minutes hunting for a good explanation. I ask Claude or ChatGPT to explain it at different levels of abstraction until it clicks.

What I am careful about is using AI to skip the actual thinking. It is very easy to ask an AI to solve a problem set, copy the answer, and feel like you learned something. You did not. The way I use it is to check my reasoning after I have attempted a problem, not before.

What AI Cannot Replace

AI makes you faster at the things you already understand, but it does not give you the judgment to know what to build or why. At LTIMindtree, the hardest problems were never about writing a function. They were about architecture decisions, predicting failure modes, and understanding the business context behind every technical choice. That kind of judgment comes from experience and from understanding systems deeply.

My Honest Take

Use AI tools aggressively to eliminate the tedious parts of your job. Writing repetitive test cases, generating migration scripts, reformatting code, summarizing documentation. Spend the time you save on the things it cannot do: thinking through architecture, reviewing code critically, and building the kind of intuition that only comes from shipping real software.
